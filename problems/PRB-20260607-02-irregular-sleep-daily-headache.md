# Problem Summary
My sleep timing varies day to day, and I am getting a headache every day. The two are almost certainly linked: irregular sleep is producing sleep debt, circadian drift, and dehydration, which together drive daily tension/morning headaches.

# Deeper Issue
Discipline (with a health consequence). The surface symptom is a headache, but the underlying layer is the absence of a stable daily structure around sleep — fixed wake time, wind-down, screens, caffeine, water. The body is paying the bill for that missing structure.

# Root Cause Analysis
Three reinforcing loops:
1. **Circadian drift.** No fixed wake time -> sleep onset shifts later -> next-day wake shifts -> permanent low-grade jet lag. The brain never gets a stable "night."
2. **Sleep debt + light/screens.** Late screens suppress melatonin, fragment deep sleep, and you wake unrefreshed. Cumulative debt manifests as headache.
3. **Hydration + caffeine.** Overnight dehydration + afternoon caffeine fragmenting sleep is a classic silent driver of daily morning/tension headaches.

Psychology: irregular sleep often hides an avoidance pattern — staying up late is "revenge bedtime procrastination," a small rebellion for autonomy after a controlled day. Worth noticing if true.

Philosophy (Stoic / dharma): the body is the vehicle. Caring for it is not optional; it is a basic duty (svadharma of the body). Discipline here is not punishment, it is freedom from a daily headache.

# Relevant Wisdom From My Knowledge Database
KND-20260607-03 (Matthew Walker on Huberman Lab — sleep science): the action plan here is directly supported by Walker's evidence base:
- Fixed wake time is the single strongest circadian anchor.
- Caffeine should stop 8–10h before bed; late caffeine can drop deep sleep by up to 30% (≈ aging your sleep by 10–12 years).
- Sleep is "landing a plane, not a light switch" — a 60-min wind-down with screens off helps onset.
- Core body temperature must drop ~1°C to fall and stay asleep — keep the room cool.
- A worry journal 1–2h before bed cuts sleep-onset time by ~50% — on par with pharmaceuticals.
- After a bad night, **do nothing** differently (don't sleep in, nap, add caffeine, or go to bed earlier) — all of these reduce next-night sleep pressure and prolong insomnia.

# What Is In My Control
- **Fully in control:** wake-up time, wind-down hour, screen cutoff, caffeine cutoff, water intake, sleep log.
- **Partly in control:** how fast the body re-aligns (5-14 days), evening social/work demands.
- **Outside my control:** any underlying medical cause — which is why action #6 (doctor) exists as a safety net.

# Possible Solutions
Ranked most-practical first:
1. **Anchor wake time + wind-down hour** (highest leverage, lowest cost). One alarm to wake, one alarm to start winding down.
2. **Cut afternoon caffeine + hydrate** (cheap, fast feedback on headache).
3. **Sleep+headache log for 7 days** (turns guesses into data).
4. **Screen curfew 60 minutes before bed** (hardest behaviorally, biggest sleep-quality win).
5. **Doctor visit** if no improvement in 2 weeks (safety net, not first move).
6. Optional later: blackout curtains, cool room (~19°C), magnesium glycinate at night — only after the basics are in place.

# Recommended Action Plan
**Today (Day 0):** set fixed wake alarm + wind-down alarm. Start the log tonight.
**Days 1-7:** hold the fixed wake time every day (including weekends), cut caffeine after 2pm, water on waking + before bed, screens off 60 min before bed, fill the log.
**Day 7:** review the log — what time did headaches happen, what preceded them?
**Days 8-14:** keep what worked; adjust one variable based on the log.
**Day 14:** if daily headaches persist, book the doctor (action #6).
